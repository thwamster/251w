	\noindent Problem 2.4.8
	Prove the following
	\vspace{0.3cm}

	\underline{proposition:}
	If $n$ is an odd integer, then there exists an integer $k$ such that $n^2=8k+1$.

	\vspace{0.4cm}
	\fbox{proof} (Direct)
	\vspace{0.2cm}

	Let $n$ be an arbitrary odd integer.
	By the definition of odd integers, let $a$ be a particular integer so that $n = 2a + 1$.
	By taking $n$ to the power of two and substitution, we see $n^2 = (2a + 1)^2$.
	By simplification and the distributive law, we see \[n^2 = 4a^2 + 4a + 1 = 4a(a + 1) + 1.\]

	Consider the parity of the product of an odd and an even integer.
	Let $b$ and $c$ be arbitrary integers so that $b$ is even and $c$ is odd.
	By the properties of integer parity, let $d$ and $e$ be particular integers so that $b = 2d$ and $c = 2e + 1$.
	By substitution, simplification, and the distributive law, we see $bc = (2d)(2e + 1) = 4de + 2d = 2(2de + d)$.
	Let $f$ be a particular integer so that $f = 2de + d$.
	By substitution, we see $bc = 2f$.
	By the properties of even integers, we see that $bc$ is even.
	Therefore, the product of an arbitrary even integer and an arbitrary odd integer must be even.
	
	By the properties of integer parity, since $a$ and $a + 1$ are consecutive integers, we see that one of $a$ and $a + 1$ must be odd and the other must be even.
	Since the product of an even integer and an odd integer must be even, by the properties of even numbers, let $k$ be a particular integer so that $a(a + 1) = 2k$.
	By substitution and simplification, we see $n^2 = 4(2k) + 1 = 8k + 1$.
	Therefore, for all odd integers $n$, there exists an integer $k$ so that $n^2 = 8k + 1$.
	$\qed$.
